Module M01 Day 1–2

Design Thinking

The mindset behind every great product decision.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the five stages of Design Thinking: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test
  • Run a structured empathy interview without leading the participant
  • Turn raw research notes into a clear problem statement
  • Generate volume over judgment during ideation sessions
  • Know when to loop back a stage instead of marching forward

Core Concepts

Why process beats inspiration

Good design rarely starts with a clever idea — it starts with a clear understanding of a real problem. Design Thinking gives teams a repeatable way to get there, instead of relying on a single person's intuition.

The five stages, in order (and out of it)

Stages are drawn as a line but used as a loop. Teams jump back to Empathize after a failed test, or skip straight to Prototype when the problem is already well understood.

  • Empathize — interviews, shadowing, contextual inquiry
  • Define — synthesize research into a point-of-view problem statement
  • Ideate — diverge wide before converging on a direction
  • Prototype — build the smallest thing that tests the idea
  • Test — put it in front of real users, not your team

Concept Deep Dive

Design Thinking is a human-centered, iterative approach to solving problems. The five stages — Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test — are not strictly linear; teams often loop back and refine continuously.

1. Empathize — Understand the User Deeply

What happens here:

  • Conduct interviews — ask users about their experiences, pain points
  • Do shadowing — observe users in real environments
  • Perform contextual inquiry — ask questions while they perform tasks

Goal: understand

  • What users say
  • What they do
  • What they feel
  • What they struggle with

2. Define — Frame the Right Problem

What happens here:

  • Analyze notes and patterns
  • Identify common pain points
  • Create a Point of View (POV) statement

Good problem statement format:

  • "User + need + insight"
UserNeedInsight

Example

"Busy professionals need a faster way to reorder meals because they don't have time to browse menus daily." Avoid vague problems like "improve app experience" — focus on specific, user-centered problems.

👉 Output: Problem statement (POV)

3. Ideate — Generate Solutions

What happens here:

  • Brainstorm ideas individually or in teams
  • Use techniques: Crazy 8s, mind mapping, SCAMPER
  • Encourage wild ideas, then narrow to feasible ones

Key rule:

  • Quantity > quality, at first

For faster food ordering: one-tap reorder, AI-based suggestions, scheduled meals, voice ordering.

Important mindset:

  • Don't build perfect — build just enough to test

5. Test — Validate with Real Users

What happens here:

  • Observe users interacting with your prototype
  • Ask: is it intuitive? Where do they struggle?
  • Collect feedback

Important:

  • Don't defend your design — learn from failures

The Iteration Loop

Design Thinking is not linear. You might go back from Test to Prototype, redefine the problem after new insights, or re-empathize if assumptions were wrong.

Simple Real-World Flow

  1. 1Users complain food delivery is slow (Empathize)
  2. 2Define: "Users need faster repeat orders" (Define)
  3. 3Brainstorm solutions (Ideate)
  4. 4Build quick UI (Prototype)
  5. 5Test with users (Test)
  6. 6Improve — repeat

Key Takeaways

  • Focus on users, not assumptions
  • Start broad, then narrow
  • Build fast, fail fast, learn fast
  • Testing with real users is critical

Quick Concept Check

See It In Practice

Design Thinking is a non-linear, iterative process teams use to understand users, challenge assumptions, and redefine problems before building solutions.
A food delivery app team noticed riders missing turns. Instead of redesigning the map, they interviewed riders and discovered the real issue was glare on phone screens at midday — leading to a high-contrast mode, not a new map engine.
In pairs, interview your partner about how they last bought a birthday gift. Build a one-page empathy map from the conversation in 15 minutes.

Today's Exercise

5 Whys Problem Dig

Pick a frustration from this morning's commute or routine. Ask 'why' five times in a row to move from surface complaint to root cause, then write a one-sentence POV statement.

Materials

  • Sticky notes or notes app
  • A timer
  • A partner to interview

Time estimate

30 minutes

Deliverable

One POV statement + 5-Whys chain, shared in the group channel.
