Module M03 Day 3–5

User Research

Stop guessing what users want — go ask them.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish qualitative from quantitative research methods
  • Write open-ended interview questions free of leading bias
  • Run a contextual inquiry session in a user's real environment
  • Synthesize interview notes into an evidence-based persona
  • Recognize when a survey is the wrong tool for the question

Core Concepts

Pick the method, then the question

Interviews uncover the 'why', surveys quantify the 'how many', and contextual inquiry reveals the gap between what people say and what they actually do.

Personas built from evidence, not vibes

A persona is only useful if every trait traces back to a real quote or observation. Decorative personas with stock photos and invented hobbies actively mislead a team.

  • Goals — what they're trying to accomplish
  • Frustrations — where the current experience fails them
  • Behaviors — what they actually do, observed not reported
  • Quote — a real line from a transcript

Concept Deep Dive

User research replaces assumptions with evidence. The method you choose depends entirely on the question you're asking.

1. Qualitative vs. Quantitative — Pick the Right Tool

What happens here:

  • Qualitative (interviews, contextual inquiry) answers why
  • Quantitative (surveys, analytics) answers how many

Key rule:

  • A survey can't tell you why users are confused — only an interview can

Qualitative

  • Interviews
  • Contextual inquiry
  • Answers why

Quantitative

  • Surveys
  • Analytics
  • Answers how many

Example

Analytics shows 40% of users drop off at checkout (quantitative). An interview reveals they don't trust the shipping cost shown so late (qualitative).
40% drop-offShipping cost distrust

👉 Output: Knowing which method actually answers your question, instead of defaulting to a survey because it's easy

2. Interviews — Ask Without Leading

What happens here:

  • Write open-ended questions ("How did that feel?" not "Wasn't that confusing?")
  • Let silence sit — don't fill gaps for the participant
  • Capture direct quotes, not paraphrases
Ask open-endedLet silence sitCapture quotes

Example

Instead of "Don't you find this slow?" ask "Walk me through what happened when you tapped that."
"Walk me through it"

👉 Output: Unbiased insight instead of a confirmation of what you already believed

3. Contextual Inquiry — Watch, Don't Just Ask

What happens here:

  • Observe users in their real environment
  • Notice the gap between what people say and what they do

What they say

  • "I always read the terms"

What they do

  • Scrolls past in 2 seconds

Example

A user says they "always read the terms" — contextual inquiry shows they scroll past it in under 2 seconds every time.
Say vs. do gap

👉 Output: Friction points users wouldn't think to mention in an interview room

4. Personas — Built From Evidence, Not Vibes

What happens here:

  • Every trait traces back to a real quote or observation
  • Structure: Goals, Frustrations, Behaviors, Quote

Key rule:

  • No stock photos, no invented hobbies — a decorative persona actively misleads a team

Example

A persona's "Frustrations" field should quote a real transcript line, not summarize a guess.

The Research Loop

Research isn't a one-time phase — a surprising interview finding can send you back to revise your research questions, and personas get revised as new sessions contradict old assumptions.

Simple Real-World Flow

  1. 1Analytics shows a drop-off (Quantitative signal)
  2. 2Interview 5–8 users about that specific step (Qualitative)
  3. 3Watch one or two complete the task live (Contextual Inquiry)
  4. 4Synthesize into an evidence-backed persona (Personas)
  5. 5Hand findings to the Define stage of the next project

Key Takeaways

  • Match the method to the question — don't default to surveys
  • Open-ended questions reveal what closed questions hide
  • What people say and what they do are often different things
  • A persona is only as good as the evidence behind it

Quick Concept Check

See It In Practice

User research is the systematic study of target users' needs and behaviors through observation, task analysis, and other feedback methods.
A banking app assumed users wanted faster transfers. Interviews revealed the real anxiety was uncertainty about whether a transfer had gone through — leading to a status-tracking feature instead of a speed boost.
Write five open-ended interview questions about how someone manages their weekly groceries, without using the word 'app' once.

Common Questions

Research on usability patterns suggests 5 users surface roughly 85% of major issues. For broader behavioral research, aim for 8–12 before patterns stop changing.

You can run discovery research in parallel with iterating on existing flows, but avoid researching a concept you're emotionally attached to shipping — the bias shows.

Today's Exercise

Five-Question Micro Interview

Interview a classmate for 10 minutes about a routine task. Avoid yes/no questions and leading language. Capture three direct quotes.

Materials

  • Notebook or notes app
  • A willing partner
  • A recorder (optional)

Time estimate

25 minutes

Deliverable

Three verbatim quotes + one surprising finding, written up.
