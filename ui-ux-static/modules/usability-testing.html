Module M08 Day 10–11

Usability Testing

Watch people use it before you ship it.

Learning Objectives

  • Write a test plan with clear tasks and success criteria
  • Moderate a session without leading the participant toward success
  • Log findings on a rainbow spreadsheet by severity
  • Calculate and interpret a System Usability Scale (SUS) score
  • Separate a 'usability problem' from a 'feature request' in session notes

Core Concepts

A test plan is a contract with yourself

Before any session, write down exactly what task the participant will attempt and what 'success' looks like. Without it, it's easy to unconsciously help someone succeed and call it good UX.

Severity, not just frequency

Not every issue deserves equal urgency. The 'rainbow spreadsheet' method colors each finding by how severe it is, independent of how many participants hit it.

  • Critical — blocks task completion entirely
  • Major — causes serious frustration or workaround
  • Minor — slows the user down slightly
  • Cosmetic — visual only, no functional impact

Concept Deep Dive

Usability testing means watching real people use it, before you ship it — independent of opinions in a design review.

The Testing Loop

Testing isn't a final gate — a Critical finding sends the design back to Prototype, and small rounds of 5 users, repeated, beat one giant study.

Simple Real-World Flow

  1. 1Write a 3-task test plan with success criteria (Test Plan)
  2. 2Run 5 moderated sessions, staying silent except the prompt (Moderated Sessions)
  3. 3Log every finding by severity (Rainbow Spreadsheet)
  4. 4Run the SUS survey for a baseline score (SUS Scoring)
  5. 5Fix Critical/Major issues, then retest

Key Takeaways

  • Five users per round catches most major usability issues
  • Don't intervene when a participant struggles — that struggle is the data
  • Severity matters more than how many people hit an issue
  • SUS gives you a number to track improvement over time

Quick Concept Check

See It In Practice

Usability testing observes real users attempting real tasks to uncover where an interface confuses, slows, or blocks them — independent of opinions or preferences.
A checkout flow tested well in design reviews but failed in testing: 4 of 5 users missed a sticky 'Apply Coupon' link because it visually matched disabled text — a Critical finding invisible to the design team.
Write a 3-task test plan for a food delivery app (e.g. find a restaurant, apply a promo code, track an order) with a clear success criterion for each task.

Common Questions

Today's Exercise

Mini Moderated Test

Recruit one classmate. Give them a single task on an app neither of you designed. Stay silent except for the task prompt. Log every hesitation, error, or comment.
